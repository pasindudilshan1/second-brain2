# Process Guide

This guide explains how the second-brain system works in simple terms.

It is not about commands or code.

It is about what is happening in the workflow from start to finish.

## The Big Idea

You collect useful articles.

The system reads them, pulls out the main ideas, connects similar articles together, and turns everything into a small knowledge network you can explore and write from.

So instead of having a pile of saved articles, you end up with:

- clear notes for each article
- shared idea pages across many articles
- maps that show what connects to what
- writing angles you can turn into content

## What You Put In

You start by adding articles you want to learn from.

These are the raw source materials.

Think of them as the library shelf.

They stay as the original input.

## What The System Does

### 1. It reads the articles

The system goes through the saved articles and tries to understand what each one is really saying.

It does not just look at the title.

It tries to identify the main points in the article.

### 2. It pulls out the 5 most important ideas

For each article, the system creates exactly 5 key ideas.

These are the strongest points or themes from that article.

Each idea also gets a score so you can see how central that idea is in the article.

High score means:

- this idea is a major point in the article

Lower score means:

- the article mentions it, but it is not the main focus

### 3. It creates one note for that article

Each article gets its own note.

That note becomes the summary page for the article.

It usually includes:

- the article title
- the 5 key ideas
- short supporting evidence
- links to similar articles
- a possible writing angle

So one long article becomes one clean working note.

### 4. It groups repeated ideas together

If different articles talk about the same theme, the system connects them.

For example, many articles may point to ideas like:

- AI adoption in finance
- CFO leadership
- regulatory pressure
- talent shortages

Instead of repeating the same idea again and again, the system gives that idea its own page.

That page becomes a shared concept page linked to all supporting articles.

This is how the system starts becoming a network instead of a stack of files.

### 5. It connects related articles

When two or more articles share the same idea, the system links them together.

This helps you see patterns like:

- many articles are talking about the same challenge
- one idea keeps showing up across different sources
- several articles support the same writing thesis

This is useful because the value is not only inside each article.

The value is also in the overlap between them.

### 6. It builds map pages

The system creates higher-level map pages that help you navigate the network.

These pages show:

- which ideas are strongest
- which articles belong to the same cluster
- what themes keep repeating
- what topics may be worth writing about

These map pages save you from opening dozens of article notes one by one.

### 7. It suggests writing directions

Once ideas and connections are built, the system can suggest writing angles.

That means it can help answer questions like:

- What topic is strong enough to write about?
- Which articles support that idea?
- Which theme looks most repeated?
- What thesis can I build from this material?

So the process does not stop at organizing information.

It moves toward helping you create something from it.

## What You Get At The End

After processing, you usually have:

- source articles
- one note per processed article
- shared idea pages
- connection maps
- a writing board
- an index
- a log of what changed

In simple terms:

- articles become notes
- notes become ideas
- ideas become connections
- connections become writing opportunities

## A Simple Example

Imagine you save 10 articles about finance, AI, and accounting.

The system may discover that:

- 6 articles talk about AI changing finance work
- 4 articles talk about CFOs becoming more strategic
- 5 articles talk about hiring and skills gaps

Instead of leaving those as 10 separate reads, the system turns them into:

- 10 article notes
- a few shared idea pages
- map pages showing how those themes overlap
- possible article angles such as:
  "Why AI in finance is really a talent and workflow story"

That is the real purpose of the process.

It helps you move from collecting information to seeing patterns.

## What You Usually Do Next

After the processing is finished, you usually:

1. open the maps
2. look at the strongest idea clusters
3. open the best supporting article notes
4. choose a writing angle
5. draft your own piece

So the system is not just for storage.

It is for turning saved reading into usable thinking.

## What Stays Untouched

Your raw articles are the original material.

Those should stay as the source layer.

The generated notes and idea pages are the working layer built from that source.

This matters because it keeps the process clean:

- source material stays original
- generated knowledge stays separate

## When You Start Fresh

Sometimes you may want to clear the generated wiki and rebuild it from scratch.

That does not mean deleting your articles.

It just means clearing the generated notes, idea pages, and maps so you can run a fresh analysis again.

## The Whole Process In One Sentence

You save articles, the system turns them into connected ideas, and those connected ideas help you write faster and think more clearly.

## Recommended Human Routine

The easiest way to use this system regularly is:

1. collect or sync new articles
2. process them into notes and ideas
3. review the strongest clusters
4. pull out a writing angle
5. repeat as the knowledge base grows

Over time, the vault becomes more valuable because the connections get stronger.
